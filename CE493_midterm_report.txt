Topic: PVT Tracking Sensors

Key Issue and Motivation
This project focuses on the design and implementation of an all-digital PVT (Process, Voltage, and
Temperature) tracking sensor system that supports an adaptive clock distribution network.
The key challenge of this project is to accurately detect the process, voltage, and temperature
(PVT) condition of the chip in real time.
Fluctuations in PVT parameters can significantly alter transistor delay and cause timing-margin loss,
especially under high-frequency voltage droops.

Key Advantage
An adaptive clocking system that can adjust its operation dynamically based on PVT conditions–
particularly to compensate for high-frequency supply voltage droops– can improve performance
and energy efficiency while ensuring safe timing operation.

Key Solution Concept
Implement a PVT-tracking sensor, either digital or analog, that provides high-fidelity tracking of
PVT-induced delay variations. In this project, we focus on an all-digital approach using:
• Replica-path–based delay sensing (Dynamic Variation Monitor, DVM)
• Clock–data delay compensation through a tunable-length delay chain
The sensor aims to monitor voltage droops and timing margin variations in real time, providing
feedback to dynamically control the clock gating mechanism.
The design concept is inspired by the 22 nm adaptive clock distribution test chip proposed by Bowman
et al., IEEE JSSC 2013 [1].

2

Background and Related Work

High-frequency voltage droops (tens to hundreds of MHz) can cause timing margin loss and performance
degradation in processors. Prior approaches such as DVFS, analog PLL modulation, and Razor-style
timing recovery have limitations in response speed or design complexity. The work by Bowman et al.
introduced an all-digital, dynamically adaptive clock distribution that combines:
• Tunable-length delay chain for preventing path timing-margin degradation for multiple cycles
• Dynamic Variation Monitor (DVM) as an on-die voltage tracking sensor
• Clock gating to prevent timing errors during droops
Our project will build a simplified RTL version to verify these concepts digitally.

Design Architecture

The proposed test system integrates the following functional blocks:
• ClockGen: Fixed-frequency clock generator (emulating PLL output)
• Tunable-Length Delay: Configurable buffer chain to adjust clock delay and simulate voltage
sensitivity
• DVM (Dynamic Variation Monitor): Sensor that detects Vcc droop onset and triggering
adaptive clock gating
• ClockGate: Clock-gating circuit controlled by the DVM output to remove clock edges that would
otherwise degrade path timing margin
• Pipeline3Stage: Three-stage logic pipeline with LFSR input/output for data stimulation and
verification
• (Optional) NoiseInjector: Module to emulate supply voltage droop during simulation

Tunable Length Delay

“The tunable-length delay, consisting of scan-programmable transistor and interconnect delay components,
prolongs the path clock-data delay compensation during a VCC droop . . . The tunable-length delay prevents
path timing-margin degradation for multiple cycles after the VCC droop occurs to enable a sufficient
response time for dynamic adaptation.”
(Section III, left column, [1])
“. . . From Figs. 4, 5, and the simulations in Figs. 5–7, path clock-data delay compensation occurs for the
duration of the clock distribution delay . . . With FCLK of 1.0 GHz, the clock-data compensation occurs
for 2 cycles, 4 cycles, and 8 cycles for the clock distribution delays of 2 ns, 4 ns, and 8 ns, respectively.”
(Section IV, p. 912, right column, [1])
The implemented tunable-length delay structure follows the design principle described in the reference
work, where a combination of transistor-dominated and interconnect-dominated paths provides
adjustable clock distribution delays. The measured post-synthesis timing results in Figure 4 show that
the transistor delay stages achieve approximately 2 ns and 8 ns of delay, corresponding to the medium
and long transistor-dominated configurations in the reference design.

Figure 4: Measured post-synthesis skew of different tunable-delay configurations.
To further align with the paper’s four delay configurations, the 4 ns mode will be realized through
an interconnect-dominated path during physical design in Innovus. The objective is to make this
configuration primarily sensitive to interconnect RC delay rather than transistor switching delay, enabling
distinct VCC -to-delay characteristics among the four modes.
The key idea for implementing the interconnect-dominated delay in Innovus is to:
• Physically separate the u in and u out buffers to a large spatial distance.
• Route the connecting net on lower metal layers with higher resistance and capacitance.
In this work, the clock skew (SKEW ) is defined as the temporal difference between the rising edge of
the input clock (clk in) and the corresponding rising edge of the delayed output clock (clk out) after
passing through the tunable-delay path. Mathematically, it can be expressed as:

Future Schedule

6.1

Dynamic Variation Monitor

Both the ROOT and LEAF clock nodes will incorporate a DVM to detect timing-margin reduction under
supply droops. Each DVM consists of a replica critical-path delay line, two transition replica chains
(TRCs) in Figure 2 for rise and fall edges, and a comparator that checks the captured delay against a
programmable threshold. When the replica delay exceeds the threshold, the DVM asserts a droop detect
pulse indicating a VCC droop event.
The DVM output passes through a two-stage synchronizer before being used by the adaptive clock
control logic. The DVMROOT triggers the clock-gating signal when droop is detected, while the DVMLEAF
provides timing-margin telemetry for monitoring and calibration.

6.2

Adaptive Clock Control Logic

The adaptive clock control logic integrates the clock-gating unit and the glitch-safe clock switch.
A glitch-free integrated clock gate (ICG) is driven by the droop detect signal from DVMROOT to pause
the processor clock for a number of cycles, ensuring that no clock edge violates leaf timing during a droop
event.
During this gated interval, a glitch-safe two-input multiplexer switches the clock source from clk in to
the delayed path clk delay, and back after recovery. The clock select signal is fully synchronized to the
clock domain and held stable during the gated period.

6.3

Noise Injector

To emulate VCC droops during validation, we will integrate an on-chip toggling load (pseudo-random
activity via LFSR) gated by a test enable; its strength and burst length are programmable to sweep
droop magnitude/frequency.

6.4

Integration in Physical Design Flow

At the current stage, the tunable-delay, DVM, clock-gate, and glitch-safe switch logic will be integrated
around the processor clock root within the Innovus physical design flow:
clk in → ICG → glitch-safe MUX(clk in, clk delay) → clk core.
The focus will be on placing and routing these modules with realistic parasitic extraction to evaluate
post-layout timing and delay sensitivity. The DVM and clock-control blocks will be floorplanned close to
the clock root to minimize insertion delay and maintain timing correlation with the tunable-delay path.
All configuration signals such as tuning bits and DVM thresholds will be statically defined at synthesis
time.

• How to implement programmable noise injector circuit to generate Vcc droop for emulation?
A programmable on-chip noise injector can be modeled using controllable current pulses or
switched capacitive loads on the VCC rail? to emulate voltage droops. For simulation, equivalent delay perturbation on the replica path can approximate its effect.
• In practice, the clock gating circuit should be off-die, but the design should model it

in multiple timing errors in DV MROOT , the clock gate should remove clock edges for a

Although real systems place the clock-gating controller off-die, an on-chip behavioral model is
necessary to validate functional response under droop. The gate should suppress a fixed or adaptive
number of clock edges upon DV MROOT assertion.
