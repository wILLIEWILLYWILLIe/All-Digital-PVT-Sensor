Topic: PVT Tracking Sensors

Key Issue and Motivation
This project focuses on the design and implementation of an all-digital PVT (Process, Voltage, and
Temperature) tracking sensor system that supports an adaptive clock distribution network.
The key challenge of this project is to accurately detect the process, voltage, and temperature
(PVT) condition of the chip in real time.
Fluctuations in PVT parameters can significantly alter transistor delay and cause timing-margin loss,
especially under high-frequency voltage droops.

Key Advantage
An adaptive clocking system that can adjust its operation dynamically based on PVT conditions–
particularly to compensate for high-frequency supply voltage droops– can improve performance
and energy efficiency while ensuring safe timing operation.

Key Solution Concept
Implement a PVT-tracking sensor, either digital or analog, that provides high-fidelity tracking of
PVT-induced delay variations. In this project, we focus on an all-digital approach using:
• Replica-path–based delay sensing (Dynamic Variation Monitor, DVM)
• Clock–data delay compensation through a tunable-length delay chain
• Dynamic leaf-clock gating is achieved by asserting an eight-cycle gating window each time a
timing-error event occurs
The sensor aims to monitor voltage droops and timing margin variations in real time, providing
feedback to dynamically control the clock gating mechanism.
The design concept is inspired by the 22 nm adaptive clock distribution test chip proposed by Bowman
et al., IEEE JSSC 2013 [1].

2

Background and Related Work

High-frequency voltage droops (tens to hundreds of MHz) can cause timing margin loss and performance
degradation in processors. Prior approaches such as DVFS, analog PLL modulation, and Razor-style
timing recovery have limitations in response speed or design complexity. The work by Bowman et al.
introduced an all-digital, dynamically adaptive clock distribution that combines:
• Tunable-length delay chain for preventing path timing-margin degradation for multiple cycles
1

• Dynamic Variation Monitor (DVM) as an on-die voltage tracking sensor
• Clock gating to prevent timing errors during droops
Our project implement a similar architecture, further incorporating a dynamic clock-gating module.

Pipeline Processor

The LFSR continuously provides random input data to the three-stage processor. Each stage of the
processor contains 32 independent logic paths. In this design, the paths include INV chains, NAND
chains, NOR chains, XOR chains, NAND-NOR chains, MUX-INV chains, and MUX-INV-NOR-XOR
chains, spanning various lengths and drive strengths.

Except INV chains, other paths have multiple input ports. To prevent any single bit of the 32-bit LFSR
from driving excessive fan-out, two 32-bit LFSRs are used in this design–one provides the input for
the main propagation chain, while the other supplies input signals for the additional multi-input ports.

3.2.2

Tunable Length Delay

The implemented tunable-length delay structure follows the design principle described in the reference
work, where a combination of transistor-dominated and interconnect-dominated paths provides
adjustable clock distribution delays. The measured post-synthesis timing results in Figure 4 show that
the transistor delay stages achieve approximately 2 ns and 8 ns of delay, corresponding to the medium
and long transistor-dominated configurations in the reference design.
To further align with the paper’s four delay configurations, the 4 ns mode will be realized through
an interconnect-dominated path during physical design. The objective is to make this configuration
primarily sensitive to interconnect RC delay rather than transistor switching delay, enabling distinct
VCC -to-delay characteristics among the three modes. The key idea for implementing the interconnectdominated delay in post layout is to route the connecting net on lower metal layers with higher resistance
and capacitance.

3.2.3

Dynamic Variation Monitor

Both the ROOT and LEAF clock nodes will incorporate a DVM to detect timing-margin reduction under
supply droops. Each DVM consists of a replica critical-path delay line, two transition replica chains
(TRCs) in Figure 2 for rise and fall edges, and a comparator that checks the captured delay against a
programmable threshold. When the replica delay exceeds the threshold, the DVM asserts a droop detect
pulse indicating a VCC droop event.
During a Vcc-droop, the DVMROOT experiences the datapath delay change without clock-data compensation, while the DVMLEAF sees a shifted version of the distribution. As mentioned earlier, the
tunable-length delay allows precise control over the timing of the clock-data compensation occurring on
the leaf side. Figure 5 highlights the functionality of the tunable-length delay combined with DVM in
the absence of clock gating. The duration of DVMLEAF ’s clock-data compensation extends by two more
cycles due to the effort of controllable distribution delay. It also shows the shifted behaviors within the
two DVM. Additionally, the DVM output passes through a two-stage synchronizer before being used
by the adaptive clock control logic, preventing metastability.
3.2.4

Adaptive Clock Control Logic

The reference paper does not implement the clock-gating module; it treats the clock gater as an off-chip
component [1]. In contrast, our work provides a full on-chip implementation of the clock-gating module.
The droop oscillation frequency in our work falls within the 50-333MHz range, and the CPU operates
at 1GHz. Meanwhile, in CPU-application scenarios, high-frequency VCC droops typically settle within
2–5 oscillation cycles. Based on this assumption, Table 1 lists the droop periods corresponding to the
frequency range of interest.
To handle various oscillation frequencies without incurring excessive hardware overhead, this work implements an adaptive clock-gating module. The module gates the leaf clock for at least eight CPU cycles
whenever a timing-error event occurs.
For high-frequency droop in the 200–333 MHz range, each oscillation cycle spans only 3–5 CPU cycles, and
the eight-cycle gating window is triggered multiple times during a droop event, effectively ensuring that
the leaf clock remains gated throughout the entire Vcc-droop interval. For droop frequency in the 50–200
MHz range, where each oscillation cycle corresponds to 5–20 CPU cycles, the timing-error detector is

Schematic Design and Simulation

The midterm report focused on the RTL-level architecture and functional verification of the proposed
adaptive clock distribution system. At that stage, all blocks, including the 3-stage pipeline, tunable-length
delay, DVM, and clock-gating logic- were implemented in Verilog and verified using Xcelium.
However, as pointed out in the instructor’s feedback, RTL simulations cannot accurately capture the
impact of PVT variations and supply voltage droops on path delay. To correctly evaluate these effects,
the design must be transferred into a transistor-level or standard-cell-based implementation
within the Virtuoso environment, where post-layout parasitics and device models are available.
In the second half of this project, we therefore adopt the following implementation flow:
1. Synthesize the RTL using Genus to obtain a gate-level netlist based on the standard-cell library.
2. Draw clock-gateing’s netlist into Virtuoso to create schematic views.
3. Manully implement all other modules schematic, including the TRCs, DVM, tunable-length delay
circuits.
4. Model the Vcc-droop at different oscillation frequencies in Verilog-A

6. Run transient simulations in Spectre across multiple PVT corners and under time-varying VCC

4.1

DVM and Tunable Delay

Based on the synthesized gate-level netlist from Genus, we instantiated the DVM and tunable-length delay
blocks in Virtuoso using the standard-cell library. The replica critical-path chain, TRCs, and receiving
flip-flops are implemented as cascaded instances of inverters, NAND, NOR, and MUX cells, matching
the logic depth and fan-out of the RTL design. Post-layout RC extraction is enabled for the replica chain
and the tunable-delay path, making the delay sensitive to both device and interconnect variations.

Figure 6: Functional verification waveform of DVMROOT and the effects of two TRCs. Vcc droop frequency
is 50Mhz
When a Vcc droop occurs, the increased TRC latency causes a sampling failure in the receiving flip-flop.
The checker module shown in Figure 1c detects this late transition and outputs an error signal in the
following cycle. The extra cycle is used to prevent metastability, since the TRC output probably arrives
too close to the rising edge of the clock. As illustrated in Figure 6, the timing error occurs near the bottom
of Vcc droop (from point V1 to point V2), where the TRC delay is the longest. This does not contradict
the expected behavior that timing failures usually appear when Vcc starts to recover. The reason is that,
unlike the DVMLEAF , the DVMROOT receives its clock directly from the PLL and therefore dose not have
any clock-data compenstation.
The different numbers of trc1 error and trc0 error, in Figure 6, show the impact of using two TRCs. In
the first and seventh trc0 error interval, the Vcc droop magnitude is relatively small, and no trc1 error is
generated. Taking the first trc0 error as an example, trc0 propagates a rising transition (0→1), whereas
trc1 propagates a falling one (1→0). Since the 0→1 transition is slower and more sensitive to Vcc droop,
the delay of trc0 becomes larger than trc1 under a mild voltage reduction, leading to an earlier timingmargin error. This dual-TRC scheme enables both transition polarities to be exercised and monitored
every cycle, without assuming a worst-case direction in advance. As a result, it improves coverage.

compensation into an adjustable mechanism. Simulation in Figure 7 illustrates that the error reported

4.2

Adaptive Clock Control

Figure 8 compares the behavior of a simple AND-based clock gate and the integrated clock-gating (ICG)
cell. Using an AND gate to directly gate the clock courses short glitch pulses when the clk en signal
deasserts (1to0). Because this deassertion occurs slightly after the cock edge, the AND gate still passes
a short high-pulse of the clock, whose width is approximately the FF TCQ delay. This short pulse may
violate the minimum high-pulse-width requirement of the leaf flip-flops and appears as a glitch on the leaf
clock, leading to unpredictable timing behavior. The ICG cell eliminates glitches by updating the clk en
signal only when the clock is low, ensuring that each high phase of the gated clock remains a full pulse.
From a timing perspective, the gated clock signal therefore appears to respond one cycle later (either
disabling or enabling the clock).
The simulation in Figure 8b also shows that the adaptive clock-control unit gates the leaf clock for at
least eight cycles whenever a timing-error event occurs. If additional errors occur during this interval, the
gating window is automatically extended. This behavior is intentional: during a fast Vcc droop, timing
errors often appear repeatedly within a short span. For reliability, the clock remains disabled when the
Vcc droop severity is high, and it is re-enabled only after the Vcc fluctuations ease and timing errors stop
occurring consecutively.

Post Layout Simulation

To evaluate the behavior of the adaptive clocking system under different operating conditions, five transient simulations were performed across various VCC droop frequencies and tunable-delay configurations.
The droop frequency represents the periodic supply disturbance applied to VCC , while the tunable delay
corresponds to the programmed clock-distribution delay inserted by the adjustable buffer chain.
Each figure includes markers on the VCC waveform to highlight one full droop period, confirming that
the simulated VCC oscillation matches the intended droop frequency. The resulting DVM behavior, leafclock timing, and error-generation patterns clearly reflect the combined effects of droop frequency and
clock-distribution delay.
The following simulation cases were examined:
• 100 MHz droop with 2 ns delay
• 100 MHz droop with 4 ns delay
• 100 MHz droop with 8 ns delay
• 200 MHz droop with 4 ns delay
• 50 MHz droop with 4 ns delay

Across the five simulation cases, the measured VCC droop periods match the intended frequencies: 10 ns
for 100 MHz, 5 ns for 200 MHz, and 20 ns for 50 MHz. These periods are annotated directly on the VCC
waveform in each figure. The DVM error spacing and leaf-clock behavior follow these droop periods:
higher-frequency droops generate more frequent error pulses, while lower-frequency droops produce wider

Lessons Learned

Although the design passed LVS, early post-layout simulations exhibited unstable supply waveforms and
abnormally large delay variations. As shown in Figure 17a, the initial layout used very narrow VDD/VSS
rails and no power-mesh structure, which resulted in severe IR drop across the block.
We strengthened the power grid by widening the rails, adding Metal-3 VDD/VSS straps, and
inserting additional vias, as illustrated in Figure 17b. These changes eliminated the excessive delay
variation and allowed the expected droop behavior to appear in simulation, highlighting the importance
of power integrity even in small test structures.

6.3

Conclusion

This project successfully implemented an all-digital adaptive clock distribution system from schematicall
call to post-layout verification. The tunable-length delay enabled controllable clock, data compensation,
and the dual-TRC DVM reliably detected VDD droops at both root and leaf nodes. The adaptive clock
controller effectively suppressed unsafe clock edges during droop events. Through this work, we not only
reproduced the key ideas of the 22 nm adaptive clocking architecture, but also gained practical experience
with physical design, RC extraction, and power integrity issues that critically affect timing behavior under
PVT variation.
